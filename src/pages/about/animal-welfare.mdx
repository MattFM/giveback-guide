This policy explains how we decide which animal experiences we include in our database and how our users can make ethical choices when engaging in experiences that involve animals.

## Our core principle

We believe the most ethical animal experiences are those where visitors peacefully observe animals displaying natural behaviours, without being encouraged, trained, or pressured into interacting with humans.

## What we don’t include in our database

We will not include experiences that involve any of the following:

- Zoos and aquariums.
- Circuses, shows, performances, or staged encounters, particularly those where animals are forced to behave unnaturally.
- Any close contact with *wild animals*, including touching, holding, cuddling, bathing, feeding, walking with, riding, or “encounter” sessions.
- Any “meet the animals” experience that involves handling or direct interaction with wild animals.
- Feeding wild animals (directly or indirectly).
- Riding experiences of any kind.
- Any situation where wild animals are sedated, baited, lured, or restrained to bring them closer to humans for entertainment or photos.
- Experiences that encourage the consumption or sale of wild animal products.

If we have any doubts whether an experience meets our standards, we will exclude it.

## What we do include in our database

We include experiences including both wild and domesticated animals, provided they meet the following requirements.

### Wild animals: sanctuaries and rescue or rehabilitation centres

As a general rule, the only wild-animal experiences we include on Giveback Guide are *sanctuaries* and *rescue or rehabilitation centres* that prioritise animal welfare and (where appropriate) have the sufficient mechanisms to release animals back into the wild.

To be included, experiences involving wild animals must meet these additional requirements:

- **No close contact.** Visitors must not be able to touch, feed, walk with, ride, wash, clean, or otherwise handle wild animals.
- **No encouraged interaction.** Animals should not be trained or enticed to approach visitors.
- **Space and welfare.** Animals should have sufficient space to roam, rest, feed, and socialise in clean, spacious environments that are as natural as possible.
- **No staged photos.** The experience should not be designed around photo opportunities, handling, or forced proximity.
- **Clear rescue purpose.** The organisation should be able to explain how animals arrived there and why they cannot be in the wild (temporarily or permanently).
- **Release programme (where applicable).** Ideally, the organisation has a credible release programme for wild animals.
- **Staff-only areas.** We may consider experiences that allow visitors to view certain staff or behind-the-scenes areas only if the design still ensures there is no touching, feeding, or direct interaction, and animals can always avoid visitors.

### Marine life (wild animals)

Marine life is covered by the same (or similar) standards as other wild animals. 

### Domesticated animals

We may include certain *domesticated animal experiences,* including cat and dog cafés (where animals are rescued and/or are available for adoption) and sanctuaries with rescued farm animals (e.g. pigs, sheep, cows, horses, donkeys etc) when they meet the following standards:

- **Animals are rescues.** Animals must be rescued, not bought, bred, or traded for the experience.
- **Adoption-first.** Ideally, the organisation supports adoption, fostering, or rehoming.
- **Animals choose the interaction.** Visitors must not coerce contact. Animals should always be able to opt out.
- **Gentle contact only (where appropriate).** Gentle contact may be acceptable only when it is clearly animal-led, the venue explicitly permits it, and the experience is run with welfare as the priority. Visitors must never chase, pick up, restrain, or force contact.
- **Escape spaces are provided.** There must be quiet, off-limits spaces where animals can rest away from people.
- **No guarantees.** The venue should not promise visitors that animals will be present, available, or willing to interact.
- **Numbers stay low.** The number of animals should be appropriate for the space and the welfare model.

### Feeding

As a general rule, we do not encourage visitors to participate in feeding animals.

There may be some exceptions, particularly with domesticated animals, where very close supervision and guidance is provided by organisers.

Helping with food preparation may be acceptable in some contexts when it does not involve handling animals or feeding them.

## Visitor guidance

Even in places that seem “ethical”, animal welfare issues may still arise. When visiting experiences that involve animals:

- Choose observation over interaction.
- Keep a respectful distance. Do not chase animals for photos.
- Do not touch or feed wild animals, even if staff encourage it.
- For domesticated animal rescues, follow staff guidance and keep contact animal-led. Never chase, pick up, restrain, or force contact.
- Do not take selfies with wild animals or pose with animals.
- Avoid flash photography.
- Avoid any venue that offers handling, bathing, bottle-feeding, or “encounters” with wild animals.
- Do your own research before attending to ensure you’re happy with the standards.

## Our approach and ongoing review

We take animal welfare seriously and do our best to check every animal experience before we include it. We review information provided by organisers alongside visitor reviews and independent sources, where available.

That said, animal welfare standards can vary over time, and circumstances can change after we publish. If you visit an experience listed on Giveback Guide and have concerns about welfare practices, we want to hear from you so that we can take appropriate action.

Use the **Report a Problem** button at the bottom of the listing and include:

- The name and location of the experience
- What you observed (and when)
- Any relevant links or photos

We will review the information and, if needed, remove the experience from our database.

This policy is a living document and will be under constant review.
