## Our Foundation: The Five Freedoms

Every animal-related project on Giveback Guide must ensure the Five Freedoms for all 
animals in their care:

1. **Freedom from Hunger and Thirst** — Ready access to fresh water and a diet to maintain full health and vigour
2. **Freedom from Discomfort** — Providing an appropriate environment including shelter and a comfortable resting area
3. **Freedom from Pain, Injury or Disease** — Prevention or rapid diagnosis and treatment
4. **Freedom to Express Normal Behaviour** — Providing sufficient space, proper facilities and company of the animal's own kind
5. **Freedom from Fear and Distress** — Ensuring conditions and treatment which avoid mental suffering

## Absolutely Prohibited

We will not list any organisation that engages in or supports:

- **Elephant rides or shows** — These activities involve breaking the animal's spirit through abusive training
- **Tiger selfies or lion walks** — These are typically cruelly bred, drugged, or both
- **Dolphin or whale shows** — Captive marine mammals suffer severe psychological and physical harm
- **Cub petting or "orphan" animal interactions** — Often involve bred-for-tourism animals separated from mothers
- **Walking with predators** — Requires cruel training methods to render dangerous animals docile
- **Bullfighting, rodeos, or animal fighting** — Clear animal cruelty
- **Fur farming or trade** — Inherently cruel industry
- **Traditional medicine using animal parts** — Drives poaching and extinction
- **Exotic pet breeding or trade** — Fuels wildlife trafficking
- **Forced feeding or overfeeding** — Causing animal distress for products (e.g., foie gras)

## Red Flags We Watch For

During verification, we look carefully for warning signs that suggest poor welfare:

### Facility and Environment

- Inadequate space for natural movement
- Barren or inappropriate enclosures
- No enrichment or stimulation
- Poor hygiene or sanitation
- No access to shelter or shade

### Staff and Practices

- Untrained staff handling animals
- No veterinary relationship or medical records
- Evidence of rough handling or punishment
- Overcrowding
- No enrichment programs

### Business Model

- Revenue primarily from animal interactions rather than conservation work
- Breeding animals specifically for tourism
- No plan for long-term care of animals
- High turnover of animals (suggests buying/selling rather than sanctuary)
- No transparency about where animals came from

## What We Look For

### Sanctuaries and Rehabilitation Centres

Genuine sanctuaries must demonstrate:

- **Lifetime commitment** — Animals are not sold, transferred, or euthanised for convenience
- **No breeding** — Responsible sanctuaries do not breed animals
- **No commercial exploitation** — Primary income should not come from animal interactions
- **Species-appropriate care** — Enclosures, diet, and social groups meet species-specific needs
- **Rehabilitation focus** — Where appropriate, genuine effort to return animals to wild
- **Transparency** — Open about animal origins, care practices, and limitations

### Conservation Projects

Wildlife conservation work must show:

- **Scientific methodology** — Research-based approaches with measurable outcomes
- **Minimal interference** — Observation and study methods that don't disturb natural behaviour
- **Local community involvement** — Conservation that benefits and involves local people
- **Long-term commitment** — Sustainable conservation, not one-off projects
- **Habitat protection** — Addressing root causes, not just symptoms

### Working Animal Projects

For projects involving working animals (e.g., search and rescue dogs, therapy animals):

- **Fair workload** — Animals have adequate rest, aren't overworked
- **Choice and agency** — Animals can opt out; work is genuinely voluntary
- **Positive reinforcement** — No aversive training methods
- **Proper care** — Veterinary care, appropriate housing, good nutrition
- **Retirement provision** — Plan for animals when they can no longer work

## Volunteer Interactions with Animals

Projects allowing direct animal contact must have strict protocols:

- **Supervision** — Trained staff always present during interactions
- **Animal choice** — Animals can choose not to participate
- **Time limits** — Interactions are brief and structured
- **Sanitation** — Proper hygiene protocols to protect animal health
- **No forced handling** — Volunteers never restrain animals against their will
- **Education focus** — Interactions teach conservation, not just entertainment

## Documentation Requirements

To be listed, animal-related projects must provide:

- Veterinary records and care plans
- Proof of legal permits and registrations
- Documentation of animal origins
- Evidence of staff training in animal care
- Enrichment program description
- Long-term care and/or release plans

## Reporting Animal Welfare Concerns

If you visit a Giveback Guide-listed project and observe animal welfare concerns, please

will investigate thoroughly. Organisations found to violate this policy will be removed 
from our platform.

## Continuous Improvement

Animal welfare science is constantly evolving. We commit to:

- Regular review and updates of this policy
- Consultation with animal welfare experts
- Listening to feedback from volunteers and visitors
- Removing organisations that no longer meet our standards
- Supporting organisations that demonstrate continuous improvement
