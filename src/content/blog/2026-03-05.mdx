## 1. Travellers Want Their Trips to Benefit Local Communities

[VisitScotland’s 2026 trends report](https://www.visitscotland.org/research-insights/trends/trends-for-tomorrow) introduces what it calls the “Give and Break” traveller, people who actively want to leave destinations better than they found them.

The report highlights that:

* 73% of travellers want their travel spending to benefit local communities
* 53% say tourism has a positive impact when it is responsibly managed

VisitScotland frames this within what it calls the “Transformation Economy” , where **travellers are no longer satisfied with passive consumption, but instead seek experiences that create personal growth and positive impact**. 

The report explicitly notes that visitors are increasingly drawn to experiences that “contribute to local people and place,” signalling a move beyond sustainability and towards tourism as a force for good.

The shift from minimising harm to actively giving back is an indication that tourists are seeking regenerative travel, maybe without even realising.

[Booking.com’s 2026 predictions](https://news.booking.com/the-era-of-you-bookingcom-predicts-the-top-trends-defining-travel-in-2026-with-individuality-taking-center-stage/) echo this shift, describing what they call “The Era of You”, where personal values and identity shape travel decisions.

**When travellers are explicitly saying they want their money to make a difference, the travel industry really should be paying attention.**

[Giveback Guide](https://giveback.guide) is not just listening, but playing an active role in connecting travellers directly to projects, experiences and organisations where money goes straight back to the community and/or the local ecosystem.

## 2. Emotional & Meaningful Travel Is Driving Decision-Making

[BBC Travel’s “Seven Travel Trends That Will Define 2026”](https://www.bbc.co.uk/travel/article/20251201-seven-travel-trends-that-will-define-2026) identifies a strong movement toward intentional, restorative and emotionally resonant travel.

The article highlights trends such as “quietcations,” slower journeys and trips centred around cultural immersion rather than itinerary box ticking. 

It emphasises that **travellers are increasingly choosing experiences that offer calmness, depth and meaning over fast-paced sightseeing**. Rather than cramming destinations into shorter trips, travellers are prioritising experiences that feel grounding and reflective.

This signals a behavioural shift. Travel is becoming less about external validation and more about internal fulfilment.

That behavioural change creates fertile ground for purpose-driven travel, because when people start to travel for meaning, contribution can become part of the equation.

[National Geographic’s 2026 travel outlook](https://www.nationalgeographic.com/travel/article/how-travel-will-look-in-2026-biggest-travel-trends) expands on this idea by highlighting conservation-led tourism and community-centred experiences as growing trends.

The report emphasises that **travellers are increasingly drawn to experiences that support biodiversity, preserve cultural heritage and empower local communities**. It positions travel as a tool for protection and education and not just for the purposes of leisure.

When one of the world’s most respected travel publications announces that conservation and cultural preservation are increasingly considered a priority for travellers, it reinforces the message that impact is no longer peripheral, but central to the future of travel.

[Explore Worldwide’s 2026 travel trends](https://www.exploreworldwide.eu/travel-trends-2026) point to continued growth in small group travel, immersive itineraries, and culturally authentic experiences.

The report notes **increasing demand for trips that prioritise local interaction, deeper storytelling, and meaningful engagement over large-scale tourism**. It emphasises a growing appetite for “authentic experiences” delivered by local experts and communities themselves.

This shift aligns closely with the principles of regenerative travel: smaller groups, deeper engagement, and economic benefit distributed locally rather than concentrated in multinational chains and/or sent elsewhere.

Across these independent reports, the theme is consistent. People aren’t just booking trips, they’re seeking meaning.

That’s exactly what regenerative travel offers.

## 3. Personal Values Now Shape Travel Planning

As I mentioned earlier in the article, [Booking.com describes 2026 as “The Era of You”](https://news.booking.com/the-era-of-you-bookingcom-predicts-the-top-trends-defining-travel-in-2026-with-individuality-taking-center-stage/), signalling a turning point where travel is increasingly shaped by individual identity, values and lifestyle.

The report explains that **travellers are seeking trips that reflect “who they are” rather than what is traditionally thought of as popular**. Personalisation is no longer about luxury upgrades, but about value alignment. Travellers want experiences that feel intentional, emotionally resonant and representative of their beliefs.

When identity becomes a filter in the planning process, platforms that allow travellers to choose experiences based on impact and purpose become increasingly relevant (oh, hello Giveback Guide).

[Expedia Group’s UNPACK ’26 report](https://www.expedia.com/newsroom/unpack-26-expedia-hotelscom-vrbo/) highlights the growing influence of curated, experience-led decision making in travel planning.

The report outlines how **travellers are moving away from mainstream destination choices and instead are building trips around specific interests, themes and lifestyle preferences**. Accommodation, local character and unique experiences are playing a greater role in shaping itineraries.

This reinforces a broader 2026 pattern that travel is increasingly curated around meaning.

## 4. Slow & Alternative Travel Is Rising

[Byway’s 2026 travel trends](https://www.byway.travel/en-US/journal/travel-trends-2026) highlight growing interest in flight-free travel and slower journeys, driven by environmental awareness and a desire for deeper exploration.

The report emphasises that **travellers are actively seeking alternatives to short-haul flights** and are embracing rail-based and overland itineraries that allow for richer engagement with landscapes and communities.

[ABTA’s 2026 travel behaviour analysis](https://www.traveldailynews.com/statistics-trends/abta-travel-trends-for-2026-highlight-shifting-uk-travel-behaviour/) similarly notes shifts in how people are travelling, including increased openness to alternative modes of transport and more thoughtful trip planning.

This reflects a broader trend that travel is becoming less about speed and more about substance.

Slower journeys create more opportunity for meaningful engagement with local businesses, cultural initiatives and environmental projects.

Regenerative travel thrives in that space.

## 5. Global Tourism Is Growing, But So Is Responsibility

According to the [UN Tourism World Tourism Barometer (January 2026)](https://pre-webunwto.s3.eu-west-1.amazonaws.com/s3fs-public/2026-01/World_Tourism%20Barometer_Jan26_excerpt_v2.pdf), international tourist arrivals reached approximately 1.5 billion, continuing the sector’s recovery and stabilisation since the pandemic.

The report notes sustained global demand across regions, reinforcing that an appetite for travel remains strong. 

With tourism volumes stabilising and growing, **the conversation is now shifting from recovery back to responsibility**. How does the travel industry manage growing demand in ways that strengthen destinations and the communities within them?

This is precisely where regenerative travel frameworks become essential.

[Deloitte’s 2026 travel and hospitality outlook](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/insights/industry/transportation/travel-hospitality-industry-outlook.html) highlights sustainability, resilience and evolving traveller expectations as core industry priorities.

The report discusses how operators must adapt to changing consumer values, operational pressures and regulatory environments, with sustainability embedded as a long-term consideration, rather than it being a marketing add-on (boooo, greenwashing, booo!).

As the travel industry pivots toward responsible growth, consumer-facing platforms that already prioritise impact (like [Giveback Guide](https://giveback.guide)) are positioned advantageously to address the changing expectations of travellers.

## 6. Culture, Events & Local Identity Are Anchoring Travel

[Airbnb’s 2026 travel predictions](https://news.airbnb.com/2026-travel-predictions-revealed/) highlight strong interest in travel moments that are anchored to culture and trips centred around events.

The report indicates that **major cultural and sporting events are influencing destination searches and bookings**, suggesting that travellers are planning trips around shared experiences and cultural participation rather than generic sightseeing.

When travel revolves around real communities and real moments, the opportunity for meaningful local engagement increases significantly.

[British Airways’ 2026 travel guide](https://www.britishairways.com/content/travel-guide/travel-trends-2026) similarly highlights experience-led travel themes shaping consumer decisions.

As travel becomes more culturally and socially anchored, community participation becomes a central theme.
